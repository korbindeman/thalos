# Procedural Terrestrial Body Pipelines: A Survey for a Feature‑Graph, Authored+Procedural Hybrid

## TL;DR
- The user's "Terrain Feature Compiler" — a feature-first, seed-addressable, era-overprinted graph that compiles into a SurfaceField contract with renderer/physics/editor projections — is well‑aligned with the strongest line of academic work (Génevaux's hydrology construction trees, Cordonnier's uplift+stream-power graph, Galin et al.'s 2019 review, Peytavie's Arches volumetric implicits) and with industry systems that converged on similar ideas (Star Citizen Planet Tech v4/v5+Genesis "directed procedural" with biome data pools; KSP1/2's PQS+ stack of mods; Outerra's elevation+fractal refinement). The vision's main risks are not architectural but scope: stable feature IDs across rerolls, a workable BiomeMaskPlan DSL, and managing the era-overprinting time order without combinatorial explosion in the manifest.
- For a "stylized realism" KSP2 visual target on a Bevy/Rust stack, the right plan is to pair a CDLOD/UDLOD-style ground mesh path with the user's flat impostor as the far-orbit projection, bake compact cubemaps (height R16, albedo Rgba8, roughness R8, dominant-material R8Uint) from the SurfaceField, and use a precomputed Bruneton sky model for atmospheres (KSP2 itself uses precomputed Bruneton scattering and per-fragment normals derived from height — exactly the path the user already proposes). For airless bodies, swap the diffuse term to a Hapke (or Lommel‑Seeliger) BRDF with an opposition‑surge term keyed by the roughness cubemap. None of those choices is exotic; all are proven.
- The biggest concrete gaps in the current spec, drawn from comparison with the literature and prior implementations, are: (1) explicit hydrology/network features as first‑class manifest entities (Génevaux/Hnaidi/Cordonnier patterns), not just a "channel incision" generator; (2) a layered material/stratigraphy column under each biome (Cordonnier 2017; Arches' material stacks) so that erosion overprints reveal sub‑surface materials rather than recoloring; (3) authoring tooling — provenance ("which feature made this ridge?"), seed promotion/locking workflows, and inverse procedural reconstruction from edits (Schott et al. 2023 do this from DEMs); (4) atmospheric/climatic coupling so biome masks can read precipitation/temperature/flux fields rather than being purely substrate-driven; and (5) a clearly defined "fusion archetype" composition algebra so a Mars-with-active-hydrocarbon-cycle isn't a special case but a normal point in the parameter space.

## Key Findings

1. **The user's pipeline is essentially the synthesis the academic terrain community has been converging on for a decade.** Génevaux et al. (2013) introduced a construction tree of feature primitives where leaves are skeletal terrain features (rivers, ridges, valleys, lakes, roads) and internal nodes are blending/carving operators — the same shape as `FeatureManifest`. Hnaidi et al. (2010) added vector control curves as authorable feature primitives diffused into a heightfield. Cordonnier et al. (2016) generated dendritic mountain/river networks from an uplift map by solving the stream‑power equation on a planar graph, then converted that graph to a DEM by blending landform feature kernels — i.e., a planning graph compiled into a continuous field, exactly the user's "manifest → SurfaceField" projection. Galin et al.'s 2019 *Computer Graphics Forum* review ("A Review of Digital Terrain Modeling") explicitly classifies methods into procedural / physically-based simulation / example-based, and recommends hybrids that mix authoring control with simulation overprints. The user's design is a hybrid in that taxonomy.

2. **Cordonnier's 2017 SIGGRAPH paper "Authoring Landscapes by Combining Ecosystem and Terrain Erosion Simulation" is the single closest academic match for the user's "era-overprinted feature graph" idea.** It maintains layered terrain (rock / sand / humus / vegetation strata) and runs interleaved erosion and ecosystem simulation that the user can pause, edit, and resume — the bidirectional feedback between erosion and biota is exactly the dependency the user gestures at when discussing biome substrate. Their layered material column should be borrowed directly: under each `BiomeId`, store a stratigraphic stack rather than a single albedo/roughness pair, so that hydraulic, glacial, or aeolian overprints in later eras *expose* the subsurface (a key visual cue on Mars analogues, Vaelen-style worlds, and ice moons). Their 2018 thesis "Layered Models for Large Scale Time-Evolving Landscapes" is the canonical reference.

3. **Galin's group has published the rest of the toolkit the user will need.** Peytavie/Galin's *Arches* (2009) introduced a volumetric stack representation that supports overhangs, arches, caves and stratified materials with implicit reconstruction — a natural extension when heightfield biomes are insufficient (slot canyons on Vaelen, lava tubes, ice arches on Europa). Cordonnier 2018 added procedural folds. Cordonnier–Jouvet et al. (2023, "Forming Terrains by Glacial Erosion") simulates glacial cycles with a deep-learning surrogate for ice flow plus multi‑scale advection, producing U‑valleys, cirques and moraines — the cleanest published method for the user's "glacial erosion" overprint. Schott/Paris/Fournier/Guérin/Galin 2023 ("Large‑scale Terrain Authoring through Interactive Erosion Simulation," ACM TOG 42:4) is highly relevant: it builds a hierarchical *uplift construction tree* (analog to the user's feature manifest) with control/feature curves, supports inverse procedural modeling (recover the uplift tree from an input DEM — the rigorous version of "seed promotion: authored→procedural→authored"), and supports hydrologically consistent blending between terrain patches. Tzathas et al. 2024 ("Physically-based analytical erosion for fast terrain generation") is worth tracking for runtime erosion within the SurfaceField evaluator. Gaillard et al. 2019 *Dendry* is a procedural model for dendritic patterns that fits the "DLA mountain network" generator slot the user lists.

4. **Industry systems group into four architectural patterns, and the user has chosen the strongest one for a small/medium team building a stylized terrestrial sim.**
   - **Stage-pipeline (mutating)**: KSP1/KSP2 PQS+ (a stack of `PQSMod` ops mutating a quad-sphere), Sebastian Lague's tutorials, KSP planet packs (Kopernicus). Easy to author one body, hard to compose, hard to introspect, no provenance — the system the user is migrating *away* from.
   - **Voxel/SDF planet**: Dual Universe (25 cm voxels, "Honeycomb"), Astroneer, Empyrion, Star Citizen's first cave passes. Lets you have caves, overhangs, deformation, but at planet scale demands enormous streaming and compression infrastructure (Dual Universe ran continuously single-shard; the studio shut servers in 2025 partly because of operational cost). For a stylized terrestrial sim this is overkill unless deformation is gameplay.
   - **Heightfield + impostor (cube‑sphere/quadtree CDLOD)**: Outerra, Infinity/I‑Novae, Frontier's Cobra/Stellar Forge, Bevy `bevy_terrain`'s spherical example, Sebastian Lague, "acko.net Making Worlds" series. This is the pragmatic path. Outerra explicitly bakes Earth's elevation into a quadrilateralized spherical cube + wavelet-compressed dataset, then uses fractal refinement on GPU below the data resolution; tiles compute in their own local frame to keep precision (Brano Kemen blog). Bevy's `bevy_terrain` (Kuhnert) ships UDLOD on top of a Chunked Clipmap; UDLOD is a GPU-driven, screen-space-uniform tessellation that morphs CDLOD-style. UDLOD plus a per-tile sample of the user's `SurfaceField` is the obvious ground-LOD plan when the impostor projection is no longer adequate.
   - **Feature-graph / authored-procedural hybrid (the user's choice)**: Star Citizen's *Genesis* + Planet Tech v4/v5 (Frankfurt, ex‑Crytek) is the closest commercial analog. CIG describes it as "directed procedural" — artists define building blocks and rules (asset packs as biome archetypes), and Genesis applies them consistently. Planet Tech v4 introduced *Temperature and Humidity maps* that *infer* biome selection from a shared data pool — the same shape as the user's BiomeMaskPlan. v5 introduces seasonal asset effects, distance-aware shading that approximates uncloaded scatter, and "archetypes for emergent biomes ... as asset packs." CIG's claim is that planet generation should be reproducible from a small set of authored archetypes plus rules, with ops consistent across the surface — almost word-for-word the user's design. Spore and No Man's Sky are also in this family but with different emphases (Spore: implicit-surface creature/terrain blends, demoscene-style tiny generators; NMS: archetype distortion + grammars, fractal Lindenmayer plant generation, plus the "superformula" controversy that Hello Games denied using).

5. **Elite Dangerous's Stellar Forge gives the canonical pattern for the *prior* phase the user calls `TerrainPrior`**: simulate top-down from astrophysical constants, store only a 64-bit hierarchical ID per body (sector → octree layer → system → body), and re-derive everything else on demand from that ID. Frontier explicitly chose simulation from first principles (mass/composition/orbits/cratering) rather than statistical tables, and their visible body geometry is a cube-faces-as-quadtrees triangle mesh. The lesson: the user's `PlanetTerrainSpec → TerrainPrior` mapping should look like a small physics/historical simulator (mass, flux, age, ice inventory, atmosphere variant) that produces *budgets and tendencies* — not a parameter dump. Frontier's archive at 80.lv ("Generating The Universe in Elite: Dangerous") and Braben's Stellar Forge writeups are good source material.

6. **For "fusion" / exotic bodies, Space Engine's parameter-driven generator is the existence proof that a single procedural pipeline can span every terrestrial archetype.** Romanyuk's interview material (Medium "Inner Worlds I") describes constraint-based generation: physical parameters (mass, radius, temperature, atmospheric pressure, composition) flow into the generator, and the generator's job is to remain plausible across the entire parameter cube. Space Engine renders Mars-likes, Titan-likes, ice worlds, super-Earths, tidally-locked exoplanets all from one code path because biome assignment, atmospheric scattering and surface reflectance are functions of the parameter vector rather than a discrete switch. The user's `archetype enum` + intent list is more directable than Space Engine's pure-physics approach (and that's the right call for a *game* that needs readable bodies), but the lesson stands: keep the archetype as a *bias on the parameter space*, not a hard branch. A "Mars-with-active-hydrocarbon-cycle" should fall out of (low gravity, thin N₂/CH₄ atmosphere, low insolation, hydrocarbon hydrosphere variant) feeding the same biome substrate evaluator — not a special case.

7. **KSP2's stylized-realism is achievable on the user's stack with three building blocks Intercept Games and the modding community already documented.** Intercept's Dev Insights #18 (Mortoc, March 2023) explains that KSP2's terrain stack is *PQS+* — KSP1's Procedural Quad System extended with modern shading, with terrain compute-dispatched then drawn through a "Local" shader near the body and a "Scaled" shader at distance. Their atmospheric scattering uses Bruneton's precomputed scattering tables (lat/lon symmetry exploited so the LUT is parameterized only by altitude and sun angles), which is also what `Scatterer` for KSP1 (Blackrack) implements and what the user should use for atmospheres. KSP1's *Parallax* mod adds tessellated terrain shaders with biome-blended scatter (rocks, foliage); *Deferred Rendering* and *TUFX* add screen-space reflections and tone-mapping that produce the "stylized but readable" lighting feel. Blackrack's volumetric clouds (KSP1, Patreon) and Intercept's For Science cloud shells are cheap shells of detail that read "atmospheric" without volume marching for orbital views. The art-style takeaways: (a) saturated but desaturated-toward-horizon palettes, (b) very strong rim/scatter contribution at terminator, (c) cloud shells with second‑order god‑rays, (d) Hapke‑ish opposition surge on airless bodies, (e) per‑fragment normals from height + finite difference (already in the spec).

8. **Tectonics, hydrology, dunes, craters, and ice tectonics each have a canonical reference the user can pull from directly.**
   - **Tectonic plates**: Andy Gainey's "Procedural Planet Generation" / Experilous writeups, Red Blob Games "Procedural map generation on a sphere" (Patel; Voronoi regions, random-fill plate boundaries, vector-field stress for boundary classification), and the academic capstone Cordonnier et al. 2018 "Procedural Tectonic Planets" (HAL hal-02136820), which approximates subduction and collision on a sphere as a procedural rather than a physical simulation. Frozen Fractal's blog series and *World Orogen*'s plate‑+‑climate generator are good production references.
   - **Hydrology graphs**: Génevaux 2013 (drainage networks → construction tree), Hnaidi 2010 (diffusion from feature curves), Derzapf et al. 2011 ("River networks for instant procedural planets"). Implementation reference: the bmelaiths/`terrainHydrology` GitHub port and Nick McDonald's blog "Procedural Hydrology" (particle stream/pool model).
   - **Crater chronology**: Hartmann–Neukum production functions (Space Sci Rev 2001), Ivanov 2001 chronology functions, Hartmann 2008 confirmation of the Mars production function. The standard practice (and the right model for the user) is a *production function* (size–frequency distribution) plus a *chronology function* (rate over time) plus a degradation/overprinting rule where younger craters obliterate older ones. This is exactly the user's Era 2/Era 5 split between heavy bombardment and recent impacts. ESA's PANGU spacecraft simulator does this for real and uses Hapke BRDF for shading; their writeup (Rowell et al., ROE Edinburgh) is a good lunar reference.
   - **Aeolian dunes**: Werner 1995 cellular automaton (slabs, shadow zones); Baas 2002 *DECAL* extension with vegetation feedback; Pelletier 2009 for ripples. Werner's algorithm produces all four canonical dune classes (barchan, crescentic, linear, star) from a single CA — ideal as a single "dune sea" generator parameterized by wind regime and sand budget. On Titan the same algorithm has been shown to produce labyrinth/plain transitions when wind+fluvial transport are coupled (Lapôtre 2022 Stanford report).
   - **DLA**: Witten–Sander Brownian-tree DLA produces the realistic dendritic mountain look popularized by Josh's Channel YouTube essay; bharadwaj-raju's "Angled Random Walks for DLA-like Terrain" gives a tractable variant. Combining DLA at coarse scale with stream-power erosion at medium scale is a strong recipe for ridge networks that don't look noisy.
   - **Ice-shell tectonics (Europa, Enceladus, fictional)**: Schmidt 2011 *Nature* "Active formation of chaos terrain" (shallow lens model), Greenberg 1999 (melt-through), Leonard 2022 (cumulative block area distribution as morphology proxy), the 2024 hydrofracture paper (arXiv 2403.09888). For a stylized game the operational model is: a "chaos field" feature whose seed places polygonal raft fragments scaled by a power law, embedded in a textured matrix; "double ridge" and "band" features as parameterized curves analogous to Hnaidi cliffs. Treat ice tectonics as a different *biome substrate generator* under the same `HeightGenerator` peer set, not as a special pipeline.

9. **Material/texture blending tradeoffs are well-explored.** The user proposes both a continuous top-N material weight mix (in the SurfaceField contract) and a baked dominant-ID `material_cubemap` for internal use. This is the right combination: dominant-ID is what production engines (Star Citizen v4/v5, KSP1 Parallax) need at low LOD because it lets you skip blending and stamp a single tiled texture, while the top-N mix preserves the gradient information needed for ground LOD and avoids the "stained-glass" boundary look of pure dominant-ID lookup. *Microsplat* in Unity (and `bevy_mesh_terrain`'s splat-map RGBA convention with R/G as texture indices and B as blend weight) is a working example of "two textures per pixel" splat that's very cheap and looks great when biome boundaries are smooth. For airless bodies, the roughness cubemap should drive both the GGX/Cook–Torrance specular term *and* the Hapke opposition‑surge width — that single coupling is what gives lunar-style "the surface gets bright when the sun is behind you" without a separate shader path.

10. **Outerra's Brano Kemen blog gives the most candid public discussion of an entire planet engine's evolution and is worth re‑reading end-to-end.** Specific patterns the user should borrow: (a) per-tile local coordinate frames for FP32 precision near surface; (b) wavelet-compressed elevation with on-GPU decompression for streaming; (c) fractal refinement *parameterized by elevation, slope, curvature and land-class data* — this is equivalent to evaluating the SurfaceField contract per fragment, with the biome mask scoring providing the land-class input; (d) road and crater systems integrated as feature overlays that affect dynamic tile generation, not as a separate system — strong endorsement of the user's "feature graph as the source of truth" decision.

11. **Sebastian Lague's *Procedural Moons and Planets* and *Coding Adventure: Hydraulic Erosion* are the best entry-level references** for individual subsystems but should not be load-bearing in the architecture. They demonstrate the cube-sphere quadtree, basic biome coloring and droplet erosion in a way that maps cleanly to the user's `HeightGenerator` peer variant slot, but they don't address feature identity, manifest provenance, or era ordering. The RedBlob/Patel sphere generator and Frozen Fractal series are stronger references for plate/climate coupling.

## Details

### Architectural taxonomy applied to the user's spec

| Pattern | Strength | Weakness | Borrow what |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Stage-pipeline (mutating) — KSP1/2, Sebastian Lague** | Trivial to author one body; clear visual debug | No provenance; rerolls invalidate downstream stages; era ordering is implicit and fragile | Visual feedback loop (use it as the *first* compiled projection target, as the user already does) |
| **Voxel/SDF — Dual Universe, Astroneer, Spore early prototypes** | Caves, overhangs, deformation, sub-surface gameplay | Streaming/compression cost at planet scale; LOD is hard; precision issues; high RAM | Use Arches-style implicit overlays only locally for canyons/lava tubes/arches/chaos rafts |
| **Heightfield + cubemap impostor — Outerra, I-Novae, Frontier, bevy_terrain** | Cheap, fast, scalable; LOD is well-understood (CDLOD/UDLOD); great precision tricks | No native overhangs; multi-layer materials need extra plumbing | UDLOD/CDLOD ground LOD when impostor is no longer enough; per-tile local frame; wavelet-compressed cubemap streaming for very large bake budgets |
| **Feature-graph hybrid — Star Citizen Genesis, Spore, NMS, the user's spec** | Stable identity, rerollable subtrees, semantic introspection, authoring-friendly | Requires careful manifest design; harder to debug "where did that ridge come from"; needs a real DSL | Genesis "asset pack as archetype"; Cordonnier/Schott "construction tree of uplift/feature primitives"; Génevaux "construction tree of skeletal feature operators" |

### Where the user's vision matches proven approaches

- **Separation of planning from terrain evaluation** is the dominant pattern in the academic graph-of-features family. Génevaux 2013 explicitly says the construction tree is *both analytic and continuous and can be rendered with varying levels of detail* — same as the user's SurfaceField contract.
- **Stable feature identity with seed substreams** maps onto Spore's "creature DNA" and Hello Games's seed-streams (terrain seed, color seed, behavior seed). The user's `FeatureSeed { identity, placement, shape, detail, children }` decomposition is more disciplined than either and supports independent reroll of placement vs shape vs detail; this is the right shape.
- **Era ordering** matches geological narrative practice in PANGU, ESA's lunar simulation work, and Hartmann/Neukum's stratigraphic chronology. The six eras (crust → bombardment → tectonics → hydrology/ice/sediment → recent impacts → local detail) align with both Mars chronostratigraphy (Noachian/Hesperian/Amazonian) and lunar Pre-Nectarian/Nectarian/Imbrian/Eratosthenian/Copernican.
- **Biome as substrate-process rather than climate class** is exactly the right move for non-Earth-likes and matches the "highland regolith / mare basalt / ejecta" vocabulary used in lunar mapping and the "rust dust plain / evaporite / dune sea / volcanic plain" vocabulary used in Mars geology. Star Citizen v4 already moved to "data-driven biome inference" via temperature/humidity, generalizing readily.
- **Cubemap projections as the renderer's view of the SurfaceField** is what Outerra (quadrilateralized spherical cube) and KSP2 (PQS quad-sphere) both do internally. The user's choice of (albedo Rgba8UnormSrgb / height R16Unorm / roughness R8Unorm / dominant-material R8Uint / object-space normal Rgba8Unorm) is reasonable; only consideration is to add a *flow direction* channel (Rg8 packed) if the user wants flow-mapped water, ice creep, or aeolian streaks.

### Where the vision diverges and whether the divergence is well-motivated or risky

- **Risk: implicit single-layer biome substrate.** The current spec gives each biome a stable id, material class, base albedo/roughness and a HeightGenerator stack. Cordonnier 2017 and Arches 2009 both store *layered* substrates (bedrock / regolith / fines / vegetation) so that erosion exposes lower layers. *Recommendation*: extend BiomeId to own a small stratigraphic stack (typically 2–4 layers) with thicknesses and material refs, evaluated to produce the SurfaceField material weights. This is what makes evaporite basins look correct after aeolian abrasion, what makes mare basalt rays look correct around fresh ejecta, and what gives Vaelen's "ancient wet" story a visible signature.
- **Risk: hydrology is implied rather than first-class.** The spec lists "channel incision" as a HeightGenerator and "Ancient hydrology, ice, or sedimentation" as Era 4, but doesn't yet give rivers, lakes, evaporite basins or paleo-channels stable feature IDs in the manifest. This is the biggest single architectural gap. *Recommendation*: lift hydrology to a proper feature subgraph following Génevaux 2013 (drainage tree as graph nodes, watershed polygons as faces, Rosgen-classified river segments as parameterized leaves) and Cordonnier 2016 (stream-power solve on the planar graph computes equilibrium elevation; the user can run this once at era 4 to set baseline relief). Hnaidi-style 3D control curves work as the authoring primitive — "draw this paleo-channel, system fills the rest."
- **Risk: no explicit story for inverse procedural / authoring promotion.** "Authored overrides may replace any feature seed or lock any feature at a chosen level" is correct in spirit, but Schott et al. 2023 demonstrated that you can *recover* a construction tree from a DEM by inversion. *Recommendation*: design the manifest from day one so a hand-painted region can be ingested as a "constraint primitive" feature (the way Schott uses control curves), with the rest of the tree re-solving against it. This makes "draw a basin shape, generator fills the consistent terrain" a first-class workflow rather than an afterthought.
- **Risk: no explicit climate/atmosphere coupling to biome masks.** The BiomeMaskPlan is described as caps, fBM masks, smoothsteps, weighted sums, products, clamps. To handle Earth-likes and exotic-fusion bodies, masks need to read climate fields (insolation, humidity, surface temperature, ice line). Star Citizen v4 already does this. *Recommendation*: add named *climate fields* as inputs to the BiomeMaskPlan (`stellar_flux_field`, `obliquity_seasonal_extreme`, `precipitation_proxy`, `subsurface_ice_stability`), each computed once during prior inference. For tidally-locked exoplanets the day-side/night-side asymmetry simply becomes a mask, not a special case.
- **Well-motivated divergence: era list is fixed and explicit.** Some academic systems prefer continuous time (Cordonnier 2017/2018, 2023) so erosion and ecosystem can interleave. The user's six discrete eras are a deliberate simplification that buys reproducibility, debuggability and authoring locality. This is the right call for a *game* where intent matters; just permit each era to internally run multiple sub-simulation passes (e.g., era 4 might run: deposit → stream-power solve → carve → re-deposit), so the era boundary is a checkpoint not a single op.
- **Well-motivated divergence: SurfaceField as the renderer's contract.** Decoupling generation from rendering through a typed sample API is exactly what Génevaux 2013 calls for ("analytic and continuous and can be rendered with varying levels of detail"). It also matches Bevy's `bevy_terrain` Attachments model where Height/Albedo/etc. are independent layers stored in chunked clipmaps. The contract should explicitly specify continuity properties (e.g., normal_local must be C¹ where the underlying generators are C¹) so consumers can rely on them.

### Concrete techniques to adopt

1. **Cordonnier 2016 stream-power on a planar graph** as Era 3/4's tectonic+fluvial backbone. Implement once on a coarse Delaunay over the cube-sphere; produces the dendritic ridge/valley structure that pure noise can never get right. Use `O(n)` Braun–Willett 2013 implicit scheme.
2. **Schott et al. 2023 hierarchical uplift construction tree with control/feature curves** as the *authorable* layer above (1). User edits curves; tree is re-evaluated; stream-power resolves to a new equilibrium. This is the closest published match to "authoring loop: keep good basin, reroll secondary craters."
3. **Hnaidi 2010 diffusion** for low-cost insertion of authored cliffs/ridges into otherwise procedural terrain — implementable as a multigrid Poisson solve and very GPU-friendly.
4. **Werner 1995 cellular dune model + Baas DECAL** as the dune-sea HeightGenerator. Feed it a wind-vector field from the climate prior and a sand-budget mask from the biome mask; same algorithm produces barchans (sparse sand) → crescentic (medium) → linear (bidirectional wind) → star (multi-directional wind). Substitute particle composition for Titan (hydrocarbon flocculate) without changing the algorithm.
5. **Hartmann–Neukum production+chronology functions** for crater populations, with younger-overprints-older as the manifest's deterministic resolution rule. Spatial hash craters as a "feature_buffers + feature_index" SSBO exactly as the spec already says; sample with finite-difference for normals on the height cubemap.
6. **DLA + Gaussian smoothing pyramid (Josh's Channel / bharadwaj-raju method)** as a ridge-network HeightGenerator. Pair with stream-power for the realistic look that neither produces alone.
7. **Cordonnier 2023 glacial erosion** if glaciers are in scope, or a simplified "U-valley carver along a high-relief ridge mask" if they're not. Buried-ice modeling is a separate flag that affects thermal stability and can drive landslide/scarp features.
8. **Arches 2009 implicit volumetric primitives** for local features that need overhangs (sea arches, slot canyons, lava tubes, ice arches, Europa rafts). Keep the planet heightfield-based; add Arches-style implicit features as *feature primitives* with their own footprint volume that the SurfaceField evaluator overlays inside.
9. **Cordonnier 2017 layered ecosystem-erosion** if vegetation is ever in scope. For airless/arid bodies, the layered substrate is still useful even without ecosystem feedback.
10. **Bruneton 2008 precomputed atmospheric scattering** as the only sane atmosphere for stylized realism. Already used by KSP2 and Scatterer. Adapt the LUT parameters per body (ozone-equivalent, scale heights, Rayleigh/Mie coefficients) from the atmosphere variant.
11. **Hapke BRDF with explicit opposition-surge width** for airless bodies, fed by the roughness cubemap. Lommel–Seeliger is acceptable as a simpler stand-in for Mars-likes. Keep PBR GGX for "thick atmosphere wet" Earth-like bodies. Switch on the atmosphere variant in the prior, not per-pixel.
12. **UDLOD (`bevy_terrain`, Kuhnert) on a spherified-cube quadtree** as the eventual ground-LOD path. The "spherified cube" mapping (`cuberact/godot-cuberact-planet-chunked-lod` README has the formula) gives more uniform vertex distribution than naive normalization, which helps when the same UDLOD tile machinery has to morph across the entire sphere. Plant the planet as 6 cube-face quadtrees, each rooting a UDLOD instance. This naturally extends the impostor projection.
13. **Cube-face quadtree + per-tile local frame** for FP32 precision (Outerra, Frontier). Camera-relative origin for tiles within a working-set distance.

### Authoring/editor UX patterns worth designing for now (gap in current spec)

- **Provenance overlay**: any sample of the SurfaceField returns the FeatureInstance ID(s) that contributed (top contributor + weights). Render this as a debug visualizer for "which feature created this ridge."
- **Seed promotion lifecycle**: `procedural → liked → locked → promoted-to-authored` as explicit states on FeatureInstance. "Promote a fresh ray crater" becomes a single transition that snapshots the seed, the resolved parameter sample, and the era it ran in.
- **Reroll workflow**: rerolling a feature's `detail` seed must not invalidate `placement` or `shape`, which is what the substream design guarantees. Editor surface: "Reroll → which substream" (placement / shape / detail / children / all). Match Houdini HDA-style "promote parameter to top level" so you can expose a small panel of intuition-friendly sliders for an artist.
- **Inverse procedural sketches**: integrating Schott 2023's idea, allow an artist to sketch a region in the editor and have the compiler reconstruct a feature subtree consistent with that sketch. For unlocking a creative loop, this is the single highest-value editor feature.
- **Diff/snapshot of the manifest** as JSON/RON for VCS, since the manifest is by design semantic and inspectable.

### KSP2 visual-target specifics

From Mortoc's Dev Insights #18 and KSP1/KSP2 Scatterer/Parallax/Deferred mod ecosystem:
- **Sky**: Bruneton precomputed scattering tables, parameterized by altitude and sun-angle; cheap LUT lookup at runtime. Replicate per atmosphere variant.
- **Cloud shells**: 2-D shell with animated detail noise, plus optional volumetric layer for orbital cinematic moments (Blackrack's volumetric clouds for KSP1 use a raymarched approach with temporal upscaling — high-quality but expensive). For Bevy, start with shells + Blackrack-style "physically-correct godrays in shadowed regions" as a stretch goal.
- **Terrain shading**: at distance, KSP2's "Scaled" shader is a simple lit-cubemap sphere — that's the user's impostor. At surface, they sample the same cubemap data plus per-fragment finite-difference normals — also exactly the user's plan. The opposition surge for airless bodies and the wet/specular for hydrosphere bodies are the readable visual cues.
- **Decals**: KSP2 Parallax uses tessellation + alpha-blended detail decals (Microsplat-style) over splat maps. Cheap and effective.
- **Distance fog & ozone-tinted limbs** are huge for "stylized but real" feel — the limb darkening and color shift sells the planet from orbit more than terrain detail does.
- **Tone mapping (TUFX-style ACES variant)** with deliberate slight desaturation toward the horizon and stronger saturation in mid-distance. KSP2's stylization is mostly post-process choices, not unique terrain math.

### Tradeoffs to call out explicitly

- **Feature-graph vs voxel**: voxel wins only if the player meaningfully modifies the planet at runtime. For a Kerbal-2-style game where players land on stable bodies, feature-graph is strictly better — better introspection, better authoring, much cheaper streaming. The user is right to choose feature-graph.
- **Baked vs analytic**: bake what the impostor (low-LOD) reads, evaluate analytically what ground LOD reads. Don't bake everything (you'll lose the manifold continuity that makes physics queries cheap), don't evaluate everything (you'll burn GPU re-running fBM stacks the impostor doesn't need). The user's "Compiler decides what becomes baked cubemap data and what remains analytic" is exactly right; codify the heuristic as a per-feature `bake_lod_cutoff` parameter.
- **Authored vs pure-procedural**: pure-procedural scales infinitely but lacks intent (NMS at launch was the cautionary tale; CIG explicitly chose "directed procedural" *because* of NMS lessons). Authored-only doesn't scale to many bodies. The user's hybrid with intent lists and lock granularity per feature is exactly the operating point that has won out in industry.
- **Single-material lookup vs continuous mix**: dominant-ID is needed for far LOD/impostor; continuous top-N mix is needed for ground LOD biome boundaries. The user's spec already has both — the only question is N. N=2 (one R/G splat in a single cubemap channel pair, blend in B) is the cheapest defensible choice; N=4 supports cleaner three-way boundaries at meaningful extra bandwidth. Star Citizen v5 reportedly runs ~4 across most distributions.

### Summary recommendations (priority-ordered)

1. Add **explicit hydrology features** to the manifest (drainage tree, lake polygons, paleo-channels) and run a Cordonnier-2016-style stream-power solve at the era-3/4 boundary. *Highest ROI gap.*
2. Add a **layered material column** under each BiomeId (Cordonnier 2017 / Arches), so era overprints expose subsurface materials.
3. Define a **climate-field input set** (flux, humidity proxy, ice stability, obliquity-driven seasonality) as inputs to BiomeMaskPlan; this generalizes biomes to all archetypes including fusion bodies without special cases.
4. Specify the **provenance API and seed-promotion state machine** before they grow organically. The earlier these are baked into FeatureInstance, the cheaper the editor will be.
5. Adopt **Bruneton precomputed scattering** for atmospheres (matches KSP2 and is the cheap right answer) and **Hapke BRDF** for airless bodies, with switch driven by the prior's atmosphere variant.
6. Plan the **UDLOD / cube-sphere quadtree ground-LOD path** as the next projection after the impostor; bevy_terrain's UDLOD + Chunked Clipmap is the reference implementation in the user's exact stack.
7. Borrow the **Werner-1995 dune CA**, **Hartmann–Neukum crater chronology with overprint rule**, and **DLA + Gaussian pyramid for ridges** as concrete HeightGenerator peer variants implementable in days each.

## Caveats

- KSP2 was put into maintenance / studio-shutdown territory after Take-Two's restructuring; the Mortoc Dev Insight #18 post and the Scatterer/Parallax mod authors remain the best public sources of how their visuals were achieved, but there is no recent post-mortem with deep architectural detail comparable to, say, *Horizon Zero Dawn*'s GDC talks. Treat the description here as a reasonable inference from public dev posts plus what the modding community has reverse-engineered, not as inside knowledge.
- Star Citizen Planet Tech v4/v5 and Genesis details come from CIG livestreams summarized by the Star Citizen Wiki and community publications (mmopixel, expcarry); these are not always primary sources and may overstate features that are still in development. The pattern (data-driven biome maps, archetype asset packs, "directed procedural") is well-documented; specific numbers (e.g. layer counts) should be treated as community estimates.
- No Man's Sky's "superformula" usage was publicly denied by Sean Murray in 2016; what's documented in his GDC 2017 "Building Worlds Using Math(s)" talk is grammar-based archetype distortion, L-systems for plants, and seeded substream determinism. Accounts that describe NMS as "running the superformula" are over-claiming.
- Several academic results (Cordonnier 2023 glacial erosion uses a deep-learning surrogate, Schott 2023 inverse procedural) are very new; runtime cost may be a concern for a real-time game and these are best treated as offline pre-bake or editor-time tools, not per-frame evaluators.
- The Dual Universe section reflects the studio-server-shutdown context as of August 2025 and the community myDU continuation; don't over-rely on it as a long-term reference for voxel-planet scaling.
- The biome list ("rust dust plains, evaporite basins, hydrocarbon lakes, ice tectonic fields") in the user's spec maps to specific real-planet phenomena with active research literatures; the references in Key Findings #8 are entry points, not exhaustive — for any specific exotic biome the user wants to target, a half-day of literature review on the corresponding planetary-science topic will surface the right physical drivers (e.g., for Titan dunes specifically, Lapôtre 2022 *GRL* and the Stanford summary).
- The user's "BiomeMaskPlan DSL with caps, fBM masks, smoothsteps, weighted sums, products, clamps, intermediate named scores" is identical in shape to standard signal-flow DSLs (Houdini VOPs, Substance Designer, Blender shader nodes). There is no published academic DSL specifically for planet-scale biome scoring; the user is in greenfield design space here. Prior art is industry workflow rather than papers — Houdini terrain HDAs and Star Citizen's "Genesis rules" are the closest analogs and worth studying for ergonomics, not algorithms.
