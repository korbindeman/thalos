# Civilization

## Species

Near-human. Biologically and psychologically recognizable. The differences are environmental, not fundamental:

- Slightly taller on average (lower gravity, 0.91g)
- Slightly larger lung capacity (thinner atmosphere, 0.85 atm)
- Material culture skews heavily metallic where Earth cultures developed wood, ceramic, and polymer traditions
- Deep cultural relationship with the ocean (65% ocean world, maritime civilization)

These are texture details. The people are relatable. They argue, dream, compromise, and stare at the sky wondering what's out there.

---

## Planetary Constraints That Shaped History

### Abundance: metals

The iron-rich crust puts metals within easy reach everywhere. Surface-exposed metallic outcrops are common. Metallurgy developed early. The Bronze Age bottleneck that slowed Earth's development (tin scarcity) didn't apply; iron working emerged earlier and spread faster. Industrialization was accelerated by material availability.

Controlling metal deposits was never a reliable path to power. One of Earth's major engines of inequality (monopolizing scarce strategic resources) barely functions here.

### Scarcity: arable land

Only 35% of the surface is land, and a significant fraction sits on geologically stagnant plates with poor soil. No fresh volcanic mineral cycling, no tectonic uplift creating new fertile terrain. Productive land is concentrated along the remaining active plate boundaries and on younger geological terrain from the tectonic active period (first ~2.5 Gyr).

Food production is the civilization's central challenge, not material production.

### Scarcity: fresh water

With a metal-rich, silicate-poor crust, the hydrological cycle differs from Earth's. Coastal desalination and rainwater capture developed early. Water management infrastructure became a foundational technology.

### The ocean

65% ocean coverage means maritime culture isn't a regional specialty, it's civilizational. Fishing, aquaculture, and ocean farming (kelp-equivalent, shellfish-equivalent) are major food sources. Deep-ocean resource extraction (minerals, thermal energy) developed early out of necessity. Coastal cities dominate.

## Political Development

### Early history: regional collectives

The scarcity of arable land and fresh water, combined with the abundance of metals, produced a consistent political pattern across cultures: collective resource management. Societies that developed communal land stewardship, shared water infrastructure, and coordinated food distribution survived. Those that didn't, collapsed.

This is the same dynamic that produced communal irrigation systems in Mesopotamia, Egypt, and the Inca Empire, but applied planet-wide and from much earlier in development.

The result: a world that trends toward cooperative governance. Not utopian (there is conflict, corruption, inequality), but the structural pressures consistently favor collective solutions. War is expensive and destructive to the limited arable land that everyone depends on.

By the pre-industrial era, the world is organized into roughly 5-8 regional cooperative federations, culturally distinct, with well-developed diplomatic traditions.

### Unification: the radio generation

The planet's smaller size (20,000 km circumference) means pre-electronic communication covers more ground, faster. But true planetary governance requires real-time communication.

Radio changes everything. Within a generation, the federations develop global coordination on ocean resource management, weather prediction, agricultural planning, and scientific collaboration. The federations merge into a planetary governance structure: federated, distributed, more like a mature EU than a centralized state. Regional cultures and identities persist.

### The world government at game start

By the time spaceflight begins, the world government is roughly 60-80 years old:

- **Established but not ancient.** Living memory includes a pre-unified world.
- **Federated, not centralized.** Regional governments retain significant autonomy. The planetary body coordinates shared resources, science, infrastructure, and the space program.
- **Stable but not complacent.** The fundamental tensions (food vs. other spending, regional vs. planetary priorities, ocean vs. land interests) are managed, not resolved.
- **Culturally unified around the sky.** The sub-Saturn and its ocean moon have been visible for all of history. The scientific consensus that the ocean moon likely harbors life is decades old. The desire to go find out is the closest thing to a universal value.

## Culture and Aesthetics

### Relationship with the sky

The sub-Saturn dominates cultural history. It swells and shrinks on a 3-year cycle, changes apparent color, eclipses background stars, and at conjunction its rings become visible to sharp-eyed observers. Every culture has a name for it. It appears in art, religion, architecture, calendar systems.

The ocean moon is visible through early telescopes as a distinct point near the gas giant. The discovery that it has an atmosphere (spectroscopy) and liquid water (reflected light analysis) were landmark scientific moments, comparable to the Copernican revolution in cultural impact.

### Material culture

Metal everywhere. Architecture uses iron and steel where Earth uses wood and concrete. Aesthetic traditions favor metallic finishes, forged ornamentation, alloy coloration. Their equivalent of "rustic" or "natural" aesthetic involves raw metal rather than raw wood.

Ocean influence is equally strong. Coastal architecture dominates. Maritime motifs in art and design. Navigation, tide prediction, and ocean mapping are prestigious disciplines.

### Relationship with food

Food is respected, almost sacred. Waste is culturally unacceptable. Cuisine is sophisticated (scarcity drives creativity) but portions are deliberate. Agricultural science is a high-status profession.

### Relationship with the planet

They know their world is geologically declining. The scientific community understands the plate tectonic trajectory. This isn't panic (the timescale is hundreds of millions of years), but it gives the culture a subtle awareness of impermanence. The planet gave them life, but it won't last forever. This may be part of what drives them outward.

The Mars analogue, visible as a reddish point in the sky, is understood to be further along the same trajectory: a world that once had water, now losing its orbit. A memento mori in the night sky.

## The Space Program

### Motivation

Not competition. Not military advantage. The space program exists because for the entire history of this species, a world with an atmosphere and liquid water has been visible in the night sky. They've known for centuries that the moon is there. They've known for decades it has conditions for life. Now they can finally go look.

This is the civilizational project. Broad popular support, funded collectively, driven by the most profound question their species can ask.

### Constraints

The world government balances the space program against competing priorities:

- **Agricultural development**: feeding everyone remains the core challenge
- **Ocean infrastructure**: deep-ocean mining, energy, and food production are still expanding
- **Geological decline**: long-term habitability planning is a background concern

The player's tension is ambition vs. budget. How fast do you push toward the ocean moon when people still need to eat?

### Starting conditions

At game start, the civilization has:

- Theoretical rocketry and early propulsion experiments
- Orbital mechanics is well-understood (centuries of precision astronomy)
- No crewed spaceflight yet
- Strong industrial base with abundant metals
- Global communication and coordination infrastructure
- A population that broadly supports the program but debates its pace

## The Game as Narrative

The game is an open-world space exploration and colony-building sandbox. The civilization provides context and motivation, not scripted missions. The player designs their own vehicles (procedural part design, technology-gated), builds infrastructure, establishes colonies, and pushes outward through the system.

### Narrative through milestones

The story progresses through **required milestone events** that gate access to the next phase, but the player has full freedom in how and when they achieve them. Between milestones, the game is open exploration and infrastructure building.

The milestones are earned, not scripted. The player built the rockets, designed the ships, established the supply chain. When they finally dive below the ocean moon's surface and see life, that moment is powerful because of the hundred hours of infrastructure that got them there.

### Progression phases

#### Phase 1: Homeworld orbit and the Moon

The player is learning. First rockets, first orbit, first landing on the moon. All resources come from the homeworld: metals (abundant), propellant precursors, electronics. The moon is a stepping stone: telescope installations, low-gravity manufacturing experiments, training for off-world operations.

**Key milestone:** Crewed orbital flight, lunar landing.

#### Phase 2: Inner system

First interplanetary probes, then crewed missions. The sub-Saturn system is the primary target (the ocean moon draws everything toward it), but the Mars analogue offers an easier first interplanetary destination.

**Key milestone:** First probe to the sub-Saturn system, first crewed interplanetary mission.

#### Phase 3: The ocean moon

The narrative climax of the early game. Reaching the ocean moon, establishing a research station with biosecurity protocols, and the first dive. The descent through chaotic surface waves into the clear shallow water. The moment the volcanic rock reveals itself as a living reef: encrusted filter-feeders, segmented swimmers, armored grazers. An alien ecosystem, Cambrian in complexity, thriving under the light of a foreign star filtered through blue-green water.

Life in the shallows is dense: photosynthesis drives the ecosystem, and the volcanic island shelves are where sunlight, nutrients, and substrate combine. Deeper water is sparser, trending toward chemosynthetic vent communities in the abyssal zones.

**Key milestone:** Ocean moon landing, first contact with alien life.

#### Phase 4: Mars colonization and belt access

Mars becomes the strategic hub. The colony grows from a research outpost to a permanent settlement to a gateway for the outer system.

**Key milestone:** Permanent Mars base, first asteroid mining operation.

#### Phase 5: Outer system

The Jupiter-analogue and its moons. The Europa-analogue with its subsurface microbial life (a second, quieter discovery). The ringed gas giant with its Titan-analogue. The ice giants at the edge of the system.

**Key milestone:** Jupiter system base, Europa-analogue discovery, outer system exploration.

#### Phase 6: Endgame

Full system infrastructure. Resource harvesting chains spanning multiple planets and moons. Early orbital habitats: the first steps toward building self-sustaining worlds in open space. The tech tree is complete. The system map is alive with the player's presence.

**Key milestone:** First self-sustaining orbital habitat, full resource network operational.

#### Possible expansion: interstellar

The system has been explored. The infrastructure exists. The technology has matured to the point where interstellar probes, and eventually ships, are conceivable. New solar systems with different configurations, different conditions, different life. A natural extension that the base game's endgame points toward.

## Resource Economy

### What the homeworld has

- **Metals**: iron, nickel, siderophile elements. Abundant and cheap. The foundation of the industrial base.
- **Electronics precursors**: silicon and other semiconductors exist in the silicate crust.
- **Energy**: solar, geothermal (from remaining active plate boundaries and core heat), tidal, wind.
- **Labor and expertise**: the planet's entire industrial and scientific workforce, concentrated and well-coordinated under the world government.

### What the homeworld lacks

- **Arable land**: the defining scarcity. Cannot be meaningfully increased on-planet.
- **Volatiles**: water is available but under pressure. Organics and hydrocarbons are less abundant than on Earth due to the metal-rich, silicate-poor composition.
- **Platinum-group elements**: concentrated in asteroid cores rather than the planetary crust (on Earth these were delivered by late heavy bombardment; on the homeworld the metal-rich crust is mostly iron/nickel).

### Resource map by body

| Body | Key resources | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Moon (III) | Low-g manufacturing, telescope sites, helium-3 (if fusion) | Training ground, early industry |
| Sub-Saturn system (IV) | Science, biosecurity tech development | Knowledge, technology spinoffs |
| Ocean moon (IV-a) | None extracted (protected biosphere) | Pure science, cultural significance |
| Mars (V) | Water ice (propellant), staging infrastructure | Gateway, propellant depot |
| Asteroid belt (VI) | Water, organics, volatiles (C-type), platinum-group metals (M-type) | Primary resource extraction zone |
| Jupiter-analogue (VII) | H₂/He atmosphere skimming, radiation-hardened tech testing | Fuel at scale, deep space staging |
| Io analogue (VII-b) | Sulfur compounds, industrial chemistry feedstock | Specialty chemicals |
| Europa analogue (VII-a) | Water ice, science | Second life discovery, water source |
| Ganymede analogue (VII-d) | Water ice, minerals, outer system base | Outer system hub |
| Ringed giant (VIII) | Atmospheric volatiles, ring ice | Fuel, ice harvesting |
| Titan analogue (VIII moon) | Hydrocarbon lakes, organic chemistry feedstock | Organics at industrial scale |
| Ice giants (IX, X) | Atmospheric volatiles, exotic ices | Deep endgame resources |

### Supply chains

**Propellant chain:** Mars water ice -> electrolysis -> H₂/O₂ propellant. Ships refuel at Mars for inner-to-outer transfers. Later supplemented by Jupiter-system atmospheric skimming for the outer system.

**Volatiles chain:** Asteroid belt C-type mining -> water, organics, carbon compounds -> shipped to homeworld and orbital infrastructure. This directly addresses the homeworld's fundamental scarcity.

**Organics chain:** Titan-analogue hydrocarbon harvesting -> organic chemistry feedstock -> shipped inward. Late-game resource that unlocks advanced materials and closed-loop ecosystem components for orbital habitats.

**Specialty chain:** Asteroid M-type mining -> platinum-group metals -> electronics, catalysts, advanced manufacturing. Belt operations staged from Mars.

## Mars Colonists

### Who goes

The homeworld is cramped and defined. Every hectare is accounted for. The people who emigrate to Mars are the ones who find that suffocating. Not rebels or dissidents, just people with a different relationship to the horizon. On Mars, you can stand on a ridge and see a hundred kilometers of terrain no one has ever named.

They self-select: explorers, tinkerers, people who'd rather jury-rig a solution from whatever's available than wait for the optimal one to be approved. The homeworld plans everything; Mars colonists improvise.

### Gravity and physiology

Mars at 0.21g is in a difficult zone for human health. Enough to walk normally, but insufficient loading for long-term bone and cardiovascular health.

First-generation colonists use **short-radius centrifuges** in their habitats: rotating sleep modules providing 0.6-0.9g for 6-8 hours nightly. This slows but doesn't eliminate degradation. Colony architecture is distinctive: static work and living spaces connected to rotating drums.

Over time, some colonists use the centrifuges less. Children born on Mars develop differently: taller, thinner, adapted to 0.21g. Within two generations, there are people who are physiologically Martian and could never comfortably live on the homeworld. This is a one-way divergence. It fits: they didn't come here to go back.

### Culture

The Mars colonists develop a distinct identity quickly:

- **Individualist, improvisational, risk-tolerant.** The inverse of the homeworld's careful collectivism.
- **Respect for space** (physical space, room to breathe, open horizon) the way the homeworld respects food.
- **Resourceful.** Everything is repurposed, nothing is wasted, but not out of cultural reverence for thrift. Out of practical necessity and a certain pride in making things work.
- **Aware of impermanence.** They live on a world with a doomed orbit. They know this. It doesn't bother them. If anything, it's part of the appeal: building something meaningful on borrowed time.

The cultural tension with the homeworld is friendly but real. The homeworld sees Mars colonists as reckless romantics. Mars colonists see the homeworld as comfortable and cautious. Neither is wrong.

### Economic role

Mars's value is positional, not extractive:

- **Propellant production**: water ice electrolysis. The primary industry. Every outer-system mission refuels here.
- **Asteroid belt staging**: Mars is the closest inhabited base to the belt. Mining operations stage, repair, and resupply at Mars.
- **Transit hub**: the only port between inner and outer system. Salvage, repair, crew rotation.
- **Science**: geological research (ancient riverbeds, orbital evolution, the dying world as a subject of study).

Mars's eccentric orbit is an asset for logistics. Near perihelion (~1.36 AU), cheap transfers to the inner system. Near aphelion (~2.04 AU), closer to the belt and Jupiter. The colony swings between markets.

## The Ocean Moon's Fate

The civilization built the space program to find life. The player decides what happens after they find it.

There are no mechanical locks preventing exploitation of the ocean moon. No invisible walls, no "are you sure?" dialogs. The game gives the player the same tools there as anywhere else: extraction infrastructure, surface construction, industrial processing. What happens to the ecosystem is a consequence of what the player builds and how carefully they build it.

### The civilization reacts

The world government and public have expectations. They funded this program to answer the greatest question in their history. If the player treats the ocean moon as just another resource node, the collective society that made everything possible starts to fracture.

Public support erodes. Funding becomes harder to secure. Political factions that never existed before emerge: preservationists who want to shut the program down, exploitationists who argue the resources justify the cost. The consensus that built the space program weakens.

### The biology responds

The Cambrian-level ecosystem evolved in total isolation over 3 billion years. It has zero exposure to foreign biochemistry. This makes it potentially devastatingly fragile, or potentially resilient in unexpected ways (alien microbes might not even be able to interact with the local chemistry). The player doesn't know which until contamination occurs.

### Spectrum of outcomes

**Careful stewardship.** Full biosecurity protocols, contained habitats, minimal footprint. The ecosystem thrives. Technology spinoffs from biosecurity research benefit the rest of the program. The civilization is proud of what it chose not to destroy.

**Negligent expansion.** Corners cut on containment because proper biosecurity is expensive and slow. Contamination events cause local die-offs around stations. Possibly recoverable. Possibly not. A slow degradation the player might not notice until it's advanced.

**Deliberate exploitation.** The ocean moon has water, minerals, geothermal energy, a biosphere that can be harvested. A player who prioritizes their supply chain over alien life can strip-mine the island arcs, dump industrial waste, acidify the ocean. The life dies.

### Irreversibility

The game does not lecture. The sonar that used to pick up biological signatures goes quiet. The shallows that were encrusted with living architecture are bare rock. Complex multicellular life took 3 billion years to evolve. It is not coming back.

### The contrast with Mars

Mars is dying through no one's fault. Its orbit is unstable, its geology is winding down, that's physics. The ocean moon's death, if it happens, is a choice. Natural decline vs. preventable destruction. The game never states this explicitly. The player feels it.
